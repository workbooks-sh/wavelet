# Strategy — KitchenAid Artisan, 12s Meta Reels

## Brand resolution

The brief asked for a "Whirlpool stand mixer." Whirlpool Corporation
acquired KitchenAid from Hobart in 1986; KitchenAid is the actual
product brand a consumer would recognize when saying "Whirlpool stand
mixer." All creative resolves the parent → sub-brand mapping: the
product on screen is a KitchenAid Artisan in Empire Red, the wordmark
is KitchenAid, the URL is kitchenaid.com.

## Positioning

This is a *durable-icon* spot, not a feature spot. KitchenAid does not
need to defend the category — it owns it. The Artisan silhouette is
the most-recognized small appliance in American kitchens; the job of
this 12 seconds is to remind a scrolling viewer that the icon still
matters, and to plant the CTA without trying to teach a feature.

The opposite-of-this — what we explicitly avoid — is the late-2010s
"techy kitchen gadget" pattern (Drop scale partnerships, app-connected
appliances, smart-everything b-roll). KitchenAid wins on heritage, not
connectivity.

## Audience

Home bakers and serious home cooks, 28-45, on Meta Reels. They are
*considering* the purchase, not impulse-buying — the spot needs to
register as a heritage cue, not a hard pitch.

## Creative directions considered

1. **Heritage cue** (CHOSEN) — three shots, slow camera, warm domestic
   light, brand wordmark held on the close. Soundtrack is felted
   piano + strings, no percussion. Reads as Sunday-morning calm; the
   mixer is *furniture*, not a feature list.
2. *Speed reel* — quick-cut whisk-cream-flour montage with rising
   beat. Rejected: feels like a Williams-Sonoma in-store loop, lacks
   the heritage register.
3. *Founders' anecdote* — type-on-screen "Same silhouette since 1937."
   Rejected: too literal; 12 seconds is too short to set up the
   factual hook without sacrificing the visual.

## Visual register (locked)

- 9:16 vertical, framed for vertical viewing — subject mid-frame, not
  cropped at top or bottom.
- Warm domestic light. Cream marble counter, brushed-brass accents,
  copper saucepan kit blurred in background.
- 35mm prime, shallow depth of field, gentle film grain. No whip
  pans, no quick zooms, no shaky cam.
- Color grade: warm highlights (slight amber), neutral midtones, very
  slightly lifted blacks. Architectural-digest aesthetic.

## Competitive scan

- KitchenAid's own 2024-2025 social work leans heavily on hand-held
  in-kitchen documentary footage (chef ambassadors, recipe POV). We
  avoid that — POV puts the viewer behind a person, not behind the
  icon. The product needs to be the protagonist.
- Cuisinart's mixer ads default to studio-white turntable product
  shots. We avoid that — sterile white = "department-store appliance,"
  not "kitchen heirloom." Counter + window light wins.
- Smeg's small-appliance work owns the colorful-pastel-on-pastel
  category. We stay warm-neutral so we don't accidentally rhyme with
  Smeg's design language.

## Pipeline beats

1. **Scene 1 (0.0-4.0s)** — Hero. Slow push-in on the Artisan on a
   marble counter, late-afternoon amber side light. Eyebrow text rises
   in: "For the ones who still make it by hand." Red rule draws in
   under the line.
2. **Scene 2 (4.0-8.0s)** — Macro. Whisk attachment slowly folding
   whipped-cream peaks. Glossy ribbon motion. Center text fades in:
   "Built to be passed down."
3. **Scene 3 (8.0-12.0s)** — Lockup. KitchenAid wordmark large, red
   on cream. Tagline: "Made for makers." CTA: kitchenaid.com. Music
   resolves on the wordmark.

## Success criteria

- First 1.5 seconds work with sound muted (visual interest only).
- KitchenAid wordmark is visible as an INTENTIONAL typographic overlay
  in scene 3 (not just printed on the mixer).
- Visual register held across all three scenes — same lens character,
  same grade.
- Music bed resolves cleanly at 12.0s, no abrupt cut.
